DDFH, Dynamically Detect and Fix Hardness for Efficient ANNS

Overview

   In this paper, we first propose Escape Hardness, a metric to evaluate the quality of graph structure around the query. And we propose Neighboring Graph Defects Fixing(NGFix) to imporve graph connectivity in regions where queries are densly distributed.

Analysis

   To provide theoretical support, we first explore the relationship between the graph stucture and accuracy for a given query. Following previous studies, we divide the search into two phases:(1) searching from the entry point to the vicinity of the query, and (2) searching within the vicinity of the query. Greedy Search can easily reach the second pahse, but it often ends up retrieving only a subset of the nearest neighbors due to the lack of effective edges in the graph index. Therefore, we first focus on the case where the entry point is close to the query. Subsequently, since Greedy Search only explores points closest to the query, we only need to consider the graph structure in the small region around the query. To better describe the area around the query, we first define the graph structure around a query, which consists of the query’s NNs and the edges associated with them:

Design

   The analysis in above section can be summarized as follows:

  1. the difficulty of a query is largely influenced by the quality of the graph structure around it, and the difficulty varies significantly across queries. So we first propose EH to measure the quality of the graph structure around a query in this section
  2. We need to utilize historical queries to enhance graph connectivity in regions where queries are densely distributed. So we propose NGFix, using EH in combination with historical queries to guide us in fixing the defective areas of the graph.
  3. There still exist a few qeries for which the search stops in the first phase and cannot reach their vicinity, so we propose RFix to enhance the navigability of some points.

   Figure 5 illustrates the overview of NGFix. We beginby constructing a base graph(Fig. 5a). For each query qqq, we compute its exact KNN(Fig. 5b) and then calculate EH for qqq(Fig. 5c). Finally, we add additional edges in the vicinity of qqq to fix the defective regions of the graph(Fig. 5d).

Preprocess

   First, we need to construct any type of graph index as a base graph, such as HNSW, NSG, etc. Then, for each query, we need to compute its exact kNN. We have the following methods for calculating kNN:

  • Collect the queries and process them after accumulating a certain amount, and then transform the computation of exact kNN into matrix multiplication, and accelerate it using matrix multiplication optimization.
  • Utilize the previously constructued graph index to perform Greedy Search, but increases the search list size to improve accuracy of approximate kNN. This method is fater then brute-force computation, but may slightly affect the ground truth quality of the queries.

Reachability Fixing

   In the following, we discuss how to address sitations where the search terminates far from the query. We propose Reachability Fixing(RFix). The key idea of Rfix is to expand the pruning candidate set of the ANN1,qANN_{1,q}ANN1,q​ 's neighbors for searches that fail to reach the vicinity of the historical query, where the ANN1,qANN_{1,q}ANN1,q​ refers to the approximate nearest neighbor returned by the Greedy Search.
   The details are shown in Algoritm 4 and the steps are as follows: (1) Select the centroid of the base data as the entry point. For a historical query, it is difficult to gurantee that Greedy Search starting from every entry point wil reach the vicinity of the query. Therefore, we fix the entry point as the centroid, which also help reduce the length of the search path. (2) Expand the candidate neighbor set of ANN1,qANN_{1,q}ANN1,q​. First, we perform a Greedy Search to retrieve the ANN1,qANN_{1,q}ANN1,q​ to the historical query(line 2). If the search did not reach the vicinity of the query(line 3), we expand the candidate neighbors of ANN1,qANN_{1,q}ANN1,q​. First, we collect all points uuu that satisfy δ(u,q)<δ(ANN1,q,q)\delta(u,q) \lt \delta(ANN_{1,q},q)δ(u,q)<δ(ANN1,q​,q) as the extended candiate set(line 4). Then, we apply the RNG pruning strategy to this set to ensure that the angle between any two connected edges is greater than 60°(line 5-9), which disperses the edges in different directions and enhances the navigational performance of the graph. Since these edges provide important paths to reach the query, their EH is set to infinity(line 9). Finally, we add these edges to the graph using the same EH pruning strategy as in NGFix(line 10). For the computation of extended candidate neighbor set(line 4), brute-force searching would be time-consuming. Therefore, we use a greedy search with a larger search list size and check all the points visited during the search process to replace the brute-force search.
   Since a single RFix does not gurantee the search will reach the vicinity of the query, we repeat the RFix until the search can reach the query’s vicinity or the degree limit for that points is reached. As only a small number of historical queries encounter issues where the search cannot reach the query’s vicinity, RFix does not significantly increase the graph construction time or index size. Index Maintenence

   We have inreoduce how to dynamically update the graph structure based on queries. However, dynamic addition and deletion of base data is also critical issue in real-world applications. Next, we discuss how the graph index handles insertion and deletion operations on the base data.
   Insertion. To support dynamic insertion, we require a base graph structure that allows incemental updates(e.g., HNSW). When a new data point arrives, it is first inserted into the base graph following its insertion algorithm. Howerver, after a large number of insertions, the extra edges linked by NGFix and RFix may have limited impact on newly inserted points, which leads to a degratin in search performance. To address this issue, we propose a partital rebuilding strategy with the following steps: (1) For each node, randomly remove a proportion rrr (e.g., 20%) of its extra outgoing edges(will not remove the edge of base graph), and reset the EH values of the remaining edges to zero. This is because the previouslt estimiated hardness may no longer reflect the current graph structure accurately. (2) Randomly select r∣T∣r|T|r∣T∣ hsitorical queries from the set of historicaly queries TTT and apply BGFix and RFix on the updated graph. A smaller rrr leads to faster reconstruction but may result in lower graph quality.
   Deletion. The most commonly used method for deleting points is lazy deletion, where the deleted points are retained for navigation during the search process but are excluded from the final result set. This method is simple and efficient. Howerver, when a large nnumber of points are added, the deleted points in the graph can significantly prolong the search paths, resulting in degraded search performance. To address this issue, we need to completely remove the point and its associated edges from the graph. This introduces two main challenges: (1) We need to identify and remove all incoming edges to the deleted point. A trivial solution is to store all incoming edges for each point in memory, but this significantly increases memory consumption. (2) The deletion of the point may damage the structure aroud it, resulting in reduced connectivity in its neighborhood.
   For the first challenge, we adopt a lazy deletion strategy for a small number of deletions. Once the number of deleted points exceeds a certain threshold(e.g., 1% of the base data), we perform a full traversal of the graph index to remove all deleted points along with their incoming edges. For the second challenge, recall that NGFix is designed to fix degective graph structures in the vicinity of queries. Thus, we can simply treat the deleted points as a query and execute NGFix. The connectivity degradation rsulting from the point deletion will be mitigated by applying NGFix.
