FusedANN, Convexified Hybrid ANN via Attribute-Vector Fusion

Summary

   We introduce FusedANN(Fused Attribute-Vector Nearest Neighbor), a geometric framework that elevates filtering into ANN optimization constraints and introduces a convex fused space cia a Lagrangian-like relaxation. Our method jointly embeds attributes and vectors through transformer-based convexification, turing hard filters into continuous, weighted penalities that preserve top-k semantics while enbaling efficient approximate search.
   FusedANN uses a filter-centric vector indexing method, a mathematically grounded transformation, that unifies attribute filtering with vector similarity search, analogous to introducing a Lagrange multipllier into a convex objective and fusion of information signals. A unfied space where: (1) the dimensionality remains unchanged, (2) the distance ordering of elements with identical attributes is preserved, and (3) a tunable parameter increases distances between differently attributed elements.

   Hierarchical Indexing Framework. We briefly sketch the idea here. To enable efficient range queries in the fused space, we introduce a hierarchical framework of three levels in Alg. 2.

  1. Leveraing the guaranteed sample complexity, which is derived from the data pattern and range distributions, we strategically takes a sufficiently large sample from the scope of possible range queries(Fig. 3a). This approache ensures that any potential query line will closely match a pre-indexed line, which minimizing storage and accounting for the varying importance of regions in the fused space.
  1. We build a specialized line similarity index that efficiently identifies the pre-indexed line most similar to LQL_{Q}LQ​ (Fig. 3b). Our line similarity cmobines directional, positional, and length components to provide strong correlation with the Hasudorff distance between lines(Fig. 3c). Note that ANN indexing of a finite LQL_{Q}LQ​ within the cylinder defining the approximate range query differs from the approximate nearest-neighbor methods, which assume inifinite lines. Our line index organize lines by their direction vectors and then by their spatical locations. The directional partitioning creates anagular cells on the unit sphere with resolution vvv (Fig. 3d/e), assigning each line to a cell based on its orientation. Within each directional group, we further organize the lines using spatical indices based on their mid-points(Alg. 5). This hierarchical structure enables logarithmic time retrical of the indexed line most similar to any query line(Alg. 6).
  1. For each indexed line L∗L^{*}L∗, we construct a cylindrical index that partitions the points by cylindrical coordinates relative to L∗L^{*}L∗, allowing efficient retrieval of the most similar points(Fig. 3f). Every line segment is divided into sections of length of the radius(sub-lines), utilizing radius-based indices per section(with respect to the perpendicular distance to its respective section line segment) in a ball tree structure. This supports rapid retrieval of points whtin a certain range, while reducing excess calculations(Fig. 3e).

   Adaptive Error Compensation. When approximating a query line with a similar indexed line, adjust the search radius and candidate count to offset the error per the lines’ Hausdorff distance. Specifically, the radius of the cylinder increases with the Hausdorff distance between the query and the indexed lines, and the candidate count is scaled by a data-dependent factor reflecting local line density. The density is calcuclated by taking the ratio of the number of points containsed within a cylindrical region to the volume of that area. Therefore, in areas of higher density, more candidates must be considered to maintain an equivalent probability of identifying the actucal nearest neighbors. Thus, when building the index, we assume a maximum Hasudorff distance supported by the pre-indexed data, add it to the optimal radius, and then construct the index. At query time, if the radius for the query if below the maximum RRR, we apply these adjusted values of kkk and the serach radius to ensure robust retrieval performace.(Fig. 3e)
   Complete Range query Algorithm and Complexity. Our range query processing first transforms the query into a line segment in the fused space, the efficiently locates the most similar indexed line via a hierarchical line index in logarithmic time. The search radius and the candidae count are adjusted based on the Hausdorff distance between the query and the indexed lines. A cylinder search retrieves candidates within the adjusted radius, which are then filtered by attribute range and ranked by distance to the query.
